Passive Safety · Occupant Protection · Python
Restraint System Benchmark Study

1D lumped-mass occupant kinematics model for evaluating seatbelt and airbag parameters in frontal crashes. Implements a haversine/trapezoidal crash pulse, a piecewise seatbelt force law (slack → elastic → load-limiter plateau), and a nonlinear airbag contact model. Five restraint configurations are benchmarked against the same pulse — belt-only, early/late airbag firing, and low/high load-limiter force — with injury-proxy metrics (3ms chest clip, HIC-style head criterion) computed from each run. Key finding: belt-only restraint yields the lowest peak chest acceleration but >1.2 m forward excursion, demonstrating why airbag firing time and load-limiter level must be co-optimised rather than tuned independently.

Journal · 2025
Comparative Analysis of Surface Roughness and Tool Wear under Dry, MQL, and Cryogenic Machining Conditions in Invar 36 Turning Operations
Shetty, S. M., Marakini, V., D'Mello, G., Pai, S. P., Ashray, Dsouza, E. W., Dsouza, L. A., & Mendonca, V. (2025)
Journal of Mines, Metals and Fuels, Vol. 73(4), pp. 1097 to 1105

Invar 36 alloy is known for its exceptional stability but presents challenges due to its tendency to heat up while machining, which can result in deformation and decreased precision. This study compares surface roughness and tool wear performance during dry, Minimum Quantity Lubrication (using canola oil), and cryogenic (LN2) machining of Invar 36 alloy across varying feed rates.

Compared to dry machining, MQL reduced surface roughness and tool wear by 14.31% and 24.33% respectively. Cryogenic machining performed best overall, reducing surface roughness by 20.6% and tool wear by 37.12%, making it the preferable cooling approach for difficult to machine materials like Invar 36.
